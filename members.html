Niels Asmussen

Matrix vesicles and their role in the regulation of the growth plate

Niels Asmussen

Matrix vesicles are known to regulate the extracellular matrix of the growth plate with the enzymes and factors that are packaged in them. I am examining the microRNA that have been found to be selectively packaged within matrix vesicles and their role in regulating the growth plate. This includes exploring the potential role of 1α,25-Dihydroxy vitamin D3 in the selective packaging of microRNA by the chondrocytes to the effect that the microRNA have on the growth plate chondrocytes as regulatory agents.

Michael Berger

Engineering Biomimetic Implants

Michael Berger

There is a growing clinical need to improve implant retention rates of orthopaedic and dental implants in patients with compromised bone qualities like osteoporosis. My thesis uses metal 3D printing to produce personalized implants with complex biomimetic shapes and porosities to address these needs. My research involves discovering the mechanisms facilitating bone formation and remodeling around metal implants possessing 3D porosity as well as testing important design and fabrication parameters to create mechanically stable implants that rapidly integrate with native bone tissue.

Jingyao Deng

Osseointegration in Neuropathy

Jingyao Deng

I am working on improving osseointegration by investigating different titanium surface structures' impact on bone remodeling both in vivo and in vitro. In vivo, the main focus is to improve implant integration in compromised patients. The compromised model we created will mimic the brain damage or nervous system deficiency and will be used to evaluate the regulatory role of the nervous system in osseointegration. In vitro, I am also evaluating the regulatory role of microstructured titanium surfaces on cellular response and interaction as well as the novel bone coupling factors in regulating crosstalk between osteoblasts and osteoclasts in bone remodeling stage.

Cydney Dennis

The Role of Hormone Signaling in the Prognosis of Laryngeal Cancer

Cydney Dennis

17β-estradiol can promote the growth and development of several laryngeal carcinoma cell lines. However, due to the variability in estrogen receptor expression, laryngeal cancer is not considered estrogen-responsive cancer. ERα36 has been shown to mediate rapid, non-genomic, membrane-associated effects of 17β-estradiol in several cancer cell lines, including triple-negative HCC38 breast cancer cells and estrogen receptor-positive MCF7 breast cancer cells producing an anti-apoptotic effect. This study provides a working model for a mechanism by which estradiol promotes anti-apoptosis through membrane-associated ERα36, as seen in previous breast cancer models, suggesting that ERα36 may be a potential membrane target for drug design against breast cancer and laryngeal cancer.

Lucas Olson

Addressing the Impact of Old Age on Decellularized Skeletal Muscle and Skeletal Muscle Regeneration

Lucas Olson

The goal of my project is to gain an understanding of the role of Advanced Glycation End-Products in the aging-associated decline in skeletal muscle regenerative ability and to leverage this knowledge to augment decellularization technology. We use decellularization to procure native skeletal muscle extracellular matrix as a scaffold that supports nascent muscle fiber growth in traumatic injuries. If we address age-dependent changes to decellularized muscle, we reduce variability in our product, thus improving patient outcomes.

James Redden

Integrin Signaling Impacting Myotube Organization and Neuromuscular Development

James Redden

Understanding the role of integrin α7β1 in regenerative synaptogenesis has the potential to improve clinical strategies addressing muscle re-innervation in cases of post-traumatic injury. The purpose of this project is to elucidate integrin α7β1-related signaling involved in muscle fiber organization of the neuromuscular junction and directly translate these findings into improved functional regeneration of muscle tissue.

Kayla Scott

Using MicroRNAs to Control the Catabolic and Inflammatory Signaling Present in Osteoarthritis

Kayla Scott

MicroRNAs (miR) are short segments of RNA that bind to sections of mRNA preventing translation. Several studies have demonstrated that miRNA are regulated during the progression of osteoarthritis (OA). OA affects over 30 million adults in the United States alone. Currently, there is no cure for OA, and current treatments focus on pain management and nondrug methods for symptom relief, with severe cases resulting in total joint replacement. The degradation of the articular cartilage, coupled with aberrant signaling results in increased terminal differentiation. This creates a positive feedback loop driving the joint further and further from homeostasis. Late-stage chondrocyte differentiation ultimately leads to cell death and depletes the joints' ability to maintain the extracellular matrix. The goal of my project is to use miR treatments to maintain the pool of chondrocytes in the articular cartilage to prevent and even reverse OA progression.
